For Academic Writing Task 1, you must write a minimum of 150 words in about 20 minutes. Examiners look for a highly structured, analytical report. Apply this blueprint to every task.

1. The Introduction (1 sentence)

Your Goal: Identify the Who, What, Where, and When of the task, and use synonyms to rewrite the prompt in your own words.

For instance, if the prompt states "The graph shows US energy consumption from 1980-2030", you write: "The line graph illustrates the amount of energy consumed in the United States across six different fuel sources between 1980 and 2030."

Rule: NEVER copy the exact words from the question.

2. The Overview (1-2 sentences)

Your Goal: Summarize the big picture. Imagine someone asks what the chart is about, but you only have five seconds to answer. You would not list numbers. You would state the main trend.

For instance: "Overall, fossil fuels remained the dominant source of energy, while renewable sources showed the most significant growth rate."

Rule: Do NOT use any specific numbers, percentages, or raw data here.

3. The Body Paragraphs (Data Discussion 1 & 2)

Your Goal: Prove the claims you made in your Overview by highlighting key information and making comparisons.

Strategy: Do not describe every single line or data point one by one. Logically group the data. Use the Point of Intersection technique to split your paragraphs where two lines cross, or divide the timeline in half.

Rule: You MUST quote specific numbers, percentages, and dates accurately here.

To accurately describe a process, compare map changes, or detail chart data, you need to rely on specific vocabulary and grammatical structures. Here is the key vocabulary for each of the six visual types.

Describing Trends (Line Graphs & Bar Charts)

When describing data that changes over time, use two main grammatical patterns for variety:

  • Pattern A (Verb + Adverb): The subject + verb of change + adverb + from [Year] to [Year].
    Example: The number of researchers in Mexico rose dramatically to a high of 242.
  • Pattern B (There is/was + Adjective + Noun): There was a + adjective + noun of change + in the subject.
    Example: There was a dramatic rise in the number of researchers.

Key Vocabulary: increased, grew, rose, soared, fell, dropped, declined, plunged, steadily, gradually, sharply.

Making Comparisons (Bar Charts & Tables)

Task 1 requires you to compare data. Do not just list numbers; highlight how they relate to one another.

  • Using "Not as... as": The use of roads declined over the period, but it was not as dramatic for roads as for canals.
  • Using Proportions: Subject A was twice as high as Subject B. Subject C was a third as much as Subject D.
  • Using Linking Words: Use words like whereas, while, however, in contrast, or on the other hand.
Proportions (Pie Charts)

When describing slices of a pie chart, you must use fraction and proportion language.

  • Key Verbs: accounts for, constitutes, represents, comprises, makes up.
  • Fractions: nearly half, exactly a quarter, just over a third, a tiny minority, the vast majority.
  • Example: Coal accounted for exactly a quarter of the total energy production.
Describing a Process Diagram

When describing how a machine works or a product is made, you are describing a permanent fact. You must use the Present Simple tense combined with the Passive voice.

  • Passive Voice: The subject + is/are + past participle verb. Example: The steam is cooled down in the cooling coil.
  • Sequencing (Before/After): Before being processed, the beans are washed.
  • The Perfect Participle: Having warmed up the radiators all over the house, the water is no longer hot.
  • Present Continuous: As the hot air is escaping, the intake valve opens to let in fresh air.
Describing Maps (Changes Over Time)

When comparing maps, organize your paragraphs chronologically or geographically.

  • Verbs of Change: transformed, completely altered, modified, developed, evolved, restored, renovated, refurbished, expanded, replaced, modernised, removed.
  • Tense Selection: If both maps represent the past, use the past simple. If the map shows changes from the past up to the present day, use the present perfect (e.g., The city centre has been completely refurbished).

To understand exactly what examiners require for a high score in Academic Task 1, it is highly effective to look at actual past candidate responses and the official examiner comments that accompanied their scores.

Example 1: Describing Multiple Charts (Band 8.0)

Prompt: The charts below show the main reasons for study among students of different age groups and the amount of support they received from employers.

The first graph shows that there is a gradual decrease in study for career reasons with age. Nearly 80 percent of students under 26 years, study for their career. This percentage gradually declines by 10 to 20 percent every decade. Only 40 percent of 40-49 year olds and 18 percent of over 49 year olds are studying for career reasons in late adulthood.

Conversely, the first graph also shows that study stemming from interest increases with age. There are only 10 percent of under 26 year olds studying out of interest. The percentage increases slowly till the beginning of the fourth decade, and increases dramatically in late adulthood, reaching nearly 60 percent for the oldest demographic.

The second graph shows that employer support is maximum at approximately 60 percent for the under 26 year students. It drops rapidly to 32 percent up to the third decade of life, and then increases in late adulthood up to about 44 percent, showing renewed employer interest in training older staff.

Why this earned a Band 8.0

  • Integration and Selection: The candidate successfully summarises the key features of both charts and integrates them well.
  • Clear Trends: They do not just list numbers. They identify clear trends and support those trends with highly appropriate, selected data points from the chart.
  • Vocabulary and Grammar: They use a very good range of vocabulary concisely and precisely with a wide range of accurate, complex sentence structures.

Example 2: Describing Maps (Band 7.0)

Prompt: The two maps below show an island, before and after the construction of some tourist facilities.

The two maps show the same island while first one is before and the second one is after the construction for tourism. Looking first at the one before construction, we can see a huge island with a beach in the west. The total length of the island is approximately 250 metres.

Moving on to the second map, we can see that there are lots of buildings on the island. There are two areas of accommodation. One is in the west near the beach while the other one is in the centre of the island. Between them, there is a restaurant in the north and a central reception block, which is surrounded by a vehicle track allowing access to all facilities.

Overall, comparing the two maps, there are significant changes after this development. Not only lots of facilities are built on the island, but also the sea is used for activities.

Why this earned a Band 7.0

  • Logical Organization: The response is incredibly easy to follow because the candidate logically organizes the data geographically and chronologically. They use strong cohesive devices to transition between areas.
  • Effective Overview: The final paragraph acts as a strong overview summarizing the main point without getting lost in details.
  • Accurate Lexicon: The candidate brings in appropriate collocations and vocabulary to describe geography, such as central reception block and western accommodation units.

Example 3: Describing a Table (Band 7.0)

Prompt: A table providing details regarding the underground railway systems in six cities.

The table shows the details regarding the underground railway systems in six cities. London has the oldest underground railway systems among the six cities. It was opened in the year 1863, and it is already 140 years old. Paris is the second oldest, having opened in 1900. Los Angeles has the newest underground railway system, and was only opened in the year 2001. In terms of the size of the railway systems, London, for certain, has the largest network.

Interestingly, Tokyo, which only has 155 kilometres of route, serves the greatest number of passengers per year, at 1927 millions passengers. This is remarkably high compared to the other locations.

In conclusion, the underground railway systems in different cities vary a lot in the size of the system, the number of passengers served per year and in the age of the system.

Why this earned a Band 7.0

  • Highlighting the Unexpected: A key requirement of Task 1 is making comparisons. This candidate beautifully contrasts data by highlighting unexpected differences, such as pointing out that Tokyo has a short route but serves the greatest number of passengers.
  • Clear Paragraphing: The candidate groups similar data. Paragraph two deals with age and size, while paragraph three specifically tackles passenger numbers.
