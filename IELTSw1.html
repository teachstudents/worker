For Academic Writing Task 1, you must write a minimum of 150 words in about 20 minutes. Examiners look for a highly structured, analytical report. Apply this blueprint to every task.

1. The Introduction (1 sentence)

Your Goal: Identify the Who, What, Where, and When of the task, and use synonyms to rewrite the prompt in your own words.

Example: If the prompt says "The graph shows teenager social media use in the USA from 2020-2023", you write: "The line graph illustrates the percentage of American secondary students using various social platforms between 2020 and 2023."

Rule: NEVER copy the exact words from the question.

2. The Overview (1-2 sentences)

Your Goal: Summarize the big picture. Imagine your friend asks what the chart is about, but you only have 5 seconds to answer. You would not list numbers; you would state the main trend.

Example: "Overall, team sports were far more popular than individual sports, with soccer seeing the highest participation."

Rule: Do NOT use specific numbers or data here. Just patterns.

3. The Body Paragraphs (Data Discussion 1 & 2)

Your Goal: Prove the claims you made in your Overview by highlighting key information and making comparisons.

Strategy: Do not describe every dot on a line. Logically group the data. Use the Point of Intersection technique (where two lines cross) to split your paragraphs, or divide by time periods.

Rule: You MUST quote specific numbers, percentages, and dates accurately here.

To accurately describe data, processes, and maps, you must rely on specific vocabulary and grammatical structures that clearly convey trends, proportions, action, and transformation. Here is the key vocabulary for each of the 6 visual types.

1. Line Graphs (Trends Over Time)

  • Trend Verbs (Up): increase, rise, grow, climb, surge, skyrocket.
  • Trend Verbs (Down): decrease, fall, drop, decline, plummet, plunge.
  • Trend Verbs (Stable/Mixed): fluctuate, remain stable, plateau, level off.
  • Adverbs for Speed/Size: sharply, significantly, dramatically, gradually, steadily, slightly.
  • Prepositions: increased from X to Y, peaked at, remained stable at.

2. Bar Charts (Comparisons)

  • Comparison Language: higher than, lower than, similar to, equal to.
  • Multipliers: twice as much as, three times more than, half the amount of.
  • Superlatives: the highest, the lowest, the most popular, the least common.
  • Grouping Devices: compared to, in contrast, whereas, while, conversely.

3. Pie Charts (Proportions)

  • Proportion Language: accounts for, constitutes, represents, comprises, makes up.
  • Fractions & Sizes: nearly half, exactly a quarter, just over a third, a tiny minority, the vast majority.
  • Comparisons: the largest proportion, the smallest segment, a significant chunk.

4. Tables (Data Grid)

  • Data Description: the highest figure, the lowest amount, a significant difference between.
  • Grouping Language: similarly, in contrast, on the other hand, notably.
  • Pattern Spotting: showed an upward trend, remained consistent across all categories, experienced identical growth.

5. Maps (Changes Over Time)

  • Construction Verbs: was constructed, was built, was developed, was established.
  • Demolition Verbs: was demolished, was knocked down, was removed, was replaced by.
  • Modification Verbs: was transformed, completely altered, refurbished, modernised, expanded.
  • Location Phrases: to the north of, adjacent to, situated in the center, located on the outskirts.

6. Processes (Step-by-Step)

  • Sequencing Devices: initially, the first stage involves, subsequently, following this, the next step is, finally.
  • The Passive Voice (Mandatory): is produced, is converted, is transported, is packaged, is stored.
  • Time Markers: before being processed, after it is completed, once finished.

Common Mistakes That Lower Your Score

Task Achievement Errors:
  • Writing under 150 words leads to an automatic penalty.
  • Including personal opinions or guessing why a trend happened.
  • Describing every single data point like a robot instead of selecting key features.
  • Missing the overview paragraph entirely.
  • Putting specific numbers or data inside the overview paragraph.
Coherence & Vocabulary Errors:
  • Writing a bulleted list instead of proper paragraphs.
  • Copying words directly from the prompt instead of paraphrasing.
  • Repeating the exact same vocabulary constantly.
  • Using informal language such as kids, huge, or gonna.
  • Subject and verb agreement errors.

To understand the difference between a passing score and a high score, let us look at how the same Line Graph prompt is answered at Band 6, 7, and 8 levels.

The Prompt: The graph below shows the proportion of the population aged 65 and over in three countries (USA, UK, Japan) from 1940 to 2040 (with projections). Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.

Band 6: The Basic Report

The line graph shows the percentage of people aged 65 and above in the USA, UK, and Japan from 1940 to 2040.

Overall, the proportion of elderly people increased in all three countries over the period. Japan started with the lowest percentage but is expected to have the highest by 2040.

In 1940, the USA had about 9% of people aged 65 or older, while the UK had around 7% and Japan had only 5%. The USA and UK showed steady increases until 2000, reaching about 14% and 15% respectively. Japan's percentage remained low until 1980, then rose sharply.

By 2040, Japan is projected to have around 27% elderly population, the highest of the three. The USA and UK are expected to reach about 23% and 22%.

Why it scores a 6: It states facts mechanically. Basic comparisons are made. Vocabulary is repetitive and simple.

Band 7: The Analytical Answer

The line graph illustrates changes in the percentage of the population aged 65 and over in three nations over a 100-year period from 1940 to 2040, including future projections.

Overall, all three countries are expected to experience significant growth in their elderly populations, with Japan showing the most dramatic increase despite starting from the lowest base.

In 1940, the USA had the highest proportion of elderly citizens at approximately 9%, followed by the UK at 7% and Japan at just 5%. Both Western nations saw gradual increases until around 2000, when figures plateaued at roughly 14%. In contrast, Japan's percentage remained relatively stable until 1980 before beginning a steep upward trajectory.

Projections indicate that by 2040, Japan will have the largest elderly population at around 27%, surpassing both the USA and the UK. This represents a massive increase for Japan compared to the growth of the other two nations.

Why it scores a 7: Selects key features effectively. Groups data logically by combining Western nations. Uses strong vocabulary like plateaued and upward trajectory.

Band 8: The Masterful Response

The provided line graph delineates trends in the proportion of citizens aged 65 and above across three developed nations spanning a century from 1940 to 2040, with data beyond 2020 representing demographic projections.

Overall, while all three countries exhibit an upward trajectory in their elderly populations, Japan is anticipated to undergo the most pronounced demographic shift, transforming from the nation with the smallest proportion of senior citizens in 1940 to the highest by 2040.

In 1940, the United States led with approximately 9% of its population aged 65 or older, compared to 7% in the UK and merely 5% in Japan. Both Western nations experienced steady, parallel growth until the turn of the millennium, plateauing at approximately 15%. Japan, however, maintained a relatively flat profile until 1980, after which its elderly population began to surge exponentially.

By 2040, demographic forecasts suggest Japan will reach a remarkable 27% elderly population, substantially exceeding the projected figures for the USA and the UK. This highlights divergent aging patterns among developed economies.

Why it scores an 8: Effortless sequencing and sophisticated paragraph referencing. Wide range of precise, academic collocations such as demographic shift and surge exponentially.

Example 1: Describing Multiple Charts (Band 8.0)

Prompt: The charts below show the main reasons for study among students of different age groups and the amount of support they received from employers.

The first graph shows that there is a gradual decrease in study for career reasons with age. Nearly 80% of students under 26 years, study for their career. This percentage gradually declines by 10-20% every decade. Only 40% of 40-49 year olds and 18% of over 49 year olds are studying for career reasons in late adulthood.

Conversely, the first graph also shows that study stemming from interest increases with age. There are only 10% of under 26 year olds studying out of interest. The percentage increases slowly till the beginning of the fourth decade, and increases dramatically in late adulthood, reaching nearly 60% for the oldest demographic.

The second graph shows that employer support is maximum at approximately 60% for the under 26 year students. It drops rapidly to 32% up to the third decade of life, and then increases in late adulthood up to about 44%, showing renewed employer interest in training older staff.

Why this earned a Band 8.0

  • Integration and Selection: The candidate successfully summarises the key features of both charts and integrates them well.
  • Clear Trends: They do not just list numbers; they identify clear trends and support those trends with highly appropriate, selected data points from the chart.
  • Vocabulary & Grammar: They use a very good range of vocabulary concisely and precisely with a wide range of accurate, complex sentence structures.

Example 2: Describing Maps (Band 7.0)

Prompt: The two maps below show an island, before and after the construction of some tourist facilities.

The two maps show the same island while first one is before and the second one is after the construction for tourism. Looking first at the one before construction, we can see a huge island with a beach in the west. The total length of the island is approximately 250 metres.

Moving on to the second map, we can see that there are lots of buildings on the island. There are two areas of accommodation. One is in the west near the beach while the other one is in the centre of the island. Between them, there is a restaurant in the north and a central reception block, which is surrounded by a vehicle track allowing access to all facilities.

Overall, comparing the two maps, there are significant changes after this development. Not only lots of facilities are built on the island, but also the sea is used for activities.

Why this earned a Band 7.0

  • Logical Organization: The response is incredibly easy to follow because the candidate logically organizes the data geographically and chronologically. They use strong cohesive devices to transition between areas.
  • Effective Overview: The final paragraph acts as a strong overview summarizing the main point without getting lost in details.
  • Accurate Lexicon: The candidate brings in appropriate collocations and vocabulary to describe geography, such as central reception block and western accommodation units.

Example 3: Describing a Table (Band 7.0)

Prompt: A table providing details regarding the underground railway systems in six cities.

The table shows the details regarding the underground railway systems in six cities. London has the oldest underground railway systems among the six cities. It was opened in the year 1863, and it is already 140 years old. Paris is the second oldest, having opened in 1900. Los Angeles has the newest underground railway system, and was only opened in the year 2001. In terms of the size of the railway systems, London, for certain, has the largest network.

Interestingly, Tokyo, which only has 155 kilometres of route, serves the greatest number of passengers per year, at 1927 millions passengers. This is remarkably high compared to the other locations.

In conclusion, the underground railway systems in different cities vary a lot in the size of the system, the number of passengers served per year and in the age of the system.

Why this earned a Band 7.0

  • Highlighting the Unexpected: A key requirement of Task 1 is making comparisons. This candidate beautifully contrasts data by highlighting unexpected differences, such as pointing out that Tokyo has a short route but serves the greatest number of passengers.
  • Clear Paragraphing: The candidate groups similar data. Paragraph two deals with age and size, while paragraph three specifically tackles passenger numbers.
